Abstract
Transformer-based models have achieved remarkable performance in NLP tasks;
however, their structural characteristics—namely, the use of multiple layers and
attention heads—introduce several challenges despite significant breakthroughs in
model accuracy. To address these challenges, head pruning has emerged as a core
strategy for reducing model complexity and enhancing inference efficiency while
preserving model performance. Previous studies perform pruning by leveraging
the Head Importance Score (HIS) computed from gradients to identify and remove
low-importance heads. However, this approach may retain heads that excessively
focus on specific tokens—thereby impairing generalization—and exhibits sharp
performance drops when the pruning ratio exceeds a certain threshold. In this paper,
we propose a novel importance score, HIES (Head Importance-Entropy Score),
which integrates the HIS and Attention Entropy (AE). Experimental results on the
GLUE and SQuAD benchmarks demonstrate that HIES outperforms the HIS-only
baseline in accuracy, while also achieving greater computational efficiency in terms
of FLOPs. These findings suggest that HIES can serve as an effective core method
for pruning, preserving the original goal of model compression without sacrificing
performance stability.
1 Introduction
Recently, advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) following scaling laws have led to remarkable
performance. At the same time, the increasing demand for on-device and local deployment presents
significant challenges in computational efficiency and memory constraints. To capture complex
dependencies, these models process increasingly long contexts and utilize numerous attention heads,
which significantly increases computational and memory costs during inference. Moreover, in very
deep networks, early-layer representations may fail to propagate properly through skip connections,
exposing structural limitations of the standard Transformer architecture (Dong et al., 2021).
To address these limitations, a simple pruning method based on the HIS was proposed, yielding
substantial gains in computational efficiency. Despite its computational advantages, HIS exhibits
several limitations. First, HIS overlooks the diversity of attention distributions, potentially leading

to entropy collapse, where heads concentrate excessively on a narrow set of tokens. Second, as the
pruning ratio increases, the probability of removing heads that contain critical information also rises,
resulting in a steep accuracy drop. Third, applying a uniform pruning criterion across all layers
and tasks impedes fine-grained adjustment since different layers or tasks require distinct attention
patterns.
This work aims to address the aforementioned limitations by proposing an Entropy-Aware Pruning
Criterion that jointly accounts for inefficient head specialization—i.e., entropy collapse—which
cannot be captured by the traditional loss-sensitivity metric alone. Concretely, we first compute
the HIS to measure each head’s contribution to the overall loss, then calculate the AE to quantify
how uniformly a head distributes its attention over tokens. We normalize and combine these two
measures into a novel score, the HIES, thereby strengthening the pruning criterion. By integrating
both “information contribution” and “distributional diversity,” our proposed HIES pruning method
prevents entropy collapse and avoids the excessive removal of critical heads that plagues single-metric
approaches.
• We provide a theoretical analysis of the limitations inherent in existing pruning methods that
rely solely on loss-sensitivity metrics, highlighting their failure to detect inefficient head
specialization, such as entropy collapse, which compromises pruning effectiveness.
• To overcome these challenges, we introduce the Entropy-Aware Pruning Crite-
rion—HIES—which integrates HIS with AE to jointly capture both the heads’ contribution
to model loss and the diversity of their attention distribution.
• Extensive experiments demonstrate that HIES consistently outperforms baselines, achieving
superior accuracy and improved computational efficiency during pruning.
2 Background and Related Work
2.1 Concept of Attention Head Pruning
Attention head pruning refers to the removal of entire attention heads from a trained Transformer
model (Vaswani et al., 2017), with the goal of reducing computational overhead and model size while
maintaining task performance. Conceptually, head pruning can be viewed as masking or removing a
subset of attention heads, thereby disabling their contribution to the model’s output. Such pruning
can be performed post hoc on a fully trained model, or integrated into training. The resulting pruned
models are more efficient in terms of computational efficacy while maintaining strong performance
on downstream tasks.
2.2 Related Works
Are Sixteen Heads Really Better Than One? Michel et al. (2019) investigates the empirical
significance of individual attention heads in Transformer architectures. Their central finding is some
attention heads are redundant and can be pruned with minimal impact on task performance. They
propose a gradient-based approach—termed the Head Importance Score—to quantify each head’s
influence on model predictions. Through experiments on both machine translation (WMT14 En–Fr)
and fine-tuned BERT (MNLI), they demonstrate that up to 20% of heads in translation tasks and up
to 40% in classification tasks can be removed without significant degradation in performance.
Stabilizing Transformer Training by Preventing Attention Entropy Collapse.Zhai et al. (2023)
investigate the training dynamics of Transformers by examining the evolution of attention entropy—a
measure of the concentration of attention weights within each head. They identify a phenomenon
termed entropy collapse, characterized by pathologically low attention entropy corresponding to
highly concentrated attention scores. This collapse is consistently associated with training instability,
manifesting as oscillating loss or divergence across various architectures and tasks. To mitigate
entropy collapse, they propose σReparam, a reparameterization technique that applies spectral
normalization to all linear layers, coupled with an additional learned scalar. This approach effectively
prevents entropy collapse, promoting more stable training across diverse tasks, including image
classification, machine translation, and speech recognition.

3 Proposed Method
3.1 Head Importance Entropy Score
In existing studies, directly comparing raw HIS and AE to rank heads is inherently unfair, as the two
metrics operate on different scales.
To address this, we first apply min–max normalization to both HIS and AE, mapping their values to
the interval [0, 1]:
dHISh = HISh − min(HIS)
max(HIS) − min(HIS) , cAEh = AEh − min(AE)
max(AE) − min(AE) .
We then define theHead Importance–Entropy Score (HIES) as a weighted combination:
HIESh = αdHISh + (1 − α)cAEh, α ∈ (0, 1),
where α is a tunable hyperparameter.
We adopt min–max normalization because it makes no assumptions about the underlying distribution
of HIS or AE and remains robust even when these metrics exhibit long tails or outliers. In particular,
non-linear or unbounded transformations could distort relative magnitudes and complicate hyperpa-
rameter tuning, which is why min–max is particularly suitable for defining HIES. By rescaling both
metrics to a common bounded range, min–max provides a clear and intuitive interpretation—values
closer to zero directly correspond to higher pruning priority—while also ensuring that the combination
weight α can be chosen and reproduced straightforwardly.

3.3 Pruning Strategy Design
3.3.1 Step 1: Min–Max Normalization of HIS and AE to Compute HIES
First, we load a pretrained Transformer model and prepare a small batch of in-domain training
examples. Using the compute_his function, we attach forward and backward hooks to each self-
attention head to capture activation outputs and gradients. We then compute the gradient-based HIS
for every head. Next, we perform a forward pass with output_attentions=True and apply the
compute_attention_entropy function to obtain each head’s average AE. We then normalize both
the HIS and AE matrices into [0, 1] via min–max normalization and combine them into the HIES
using
HIESℓ,h = αdHISℓ,h + (1 − α)cAEℓ,h, α ∈ (0, 1).
3.3.2 Step 2: Head Selection Based on HIES
We flatten the L × H HIES score matrix into a list of triples (ℓ, h, HIESℓ,h) and sort this list in
ascending order of HIES. For a chosen pruning fraction p, we select the lowest ⌊p · L · H⌋ heads
as pruning candidates. These indices are grouped by layer into a prune_map, which is passed to
model.bert.prune_heads(prune_map) to remove the specified heads from the model.
3.3.3 Step 3: Comparative Evaluation of Model Performance
For the evaluation of the HIES-based pruning method, we assess model performance on the validation
set across pruning fractions ranging from 10% to 90%. To examine the robustness of our method
under extreme compression, we include high pruning rates (up to 90%). Such aggressive pruning
helps reveal failure points and stress-test the reliability of pruning criteria under severe resource
constraints. For each fraction, we reload the original model, apply the corresponding prune_map,
and measure classification accuracy. We plotV alidation Accuracy (%)against Pruning Fraction (%)
and compare results to HIS-only and AE-only baselines. Furthermore, we visualize the full HIES
heatmap and report per-head scores to highlight which heads are deemed most dispensable.
4 Evaluation Methods
4.1 Performance Metric
For classification tasks such as SST-2, MRPC, and CoLA, we evaluate model performance based on
accuracy. For the question answering task, SQuAD (v1.1), we additionally employ Exact Match (EM)
and F1 scores to capture nuances in answer span prediction under different pruning configurations.

datasets are used across all pruning methods. No retraining is performed after pruning; instead,
models are evaluated immediately to assess the intrinsic effectiveness of each pruning strategy. As

In our HIES method, we first compute raw HIS and AE scores on the fine-tuned model via for-
ward–backward passes—HIS by aggregating gradient-weighted head outputs, and AE as the negative
entropy of each head’s attention distribution. Next, we apply min–max normalization to map both

HIESh = αdHISh + (1 − α)cAEh, α ∈ (0, 1),

HIES(h)(α), evaluate on the validation set, and record the performance metric. The value of α that
yields the highest validation score (accuracy or F1/EM) is selected for each task.

5 Experimental Results
5.1 Ablation on α (HIS/AE Weight)
To validate the stability of our proposed method, we analyze its sensitivity to the weighting coefficient
α, which balances the HIS and AE in the HIES pruning criterion. As illustrated in Figure 3 of the
appendix, model performance remains stable across a wide range of α values, demonstrating that
HIES is robust to variations in this hyperparameter. Higher α values prioritize gradient-based HIS,
favoring heads with strong task-specific contributions, while lower α values emphasize AE, retaining
heads with focused attention patterns.
5.2 Accuracy/Exact Match/F1 Score vs. Pruning Fraction
Figure 1 presents a comparison of the performance of the model in different pruning strategies -
AE, HIS, and HIES - in various tasks. We first focus on the impact of the pruning strategy on task
performance. Pruning based solely on AE consistently resulted in substantially degraded performance
across all tasks compared to the other two methods. Although AE captures the diversity of attention
distributions, it does not account for each head’s actual contribution to the model loss. Consequently,
compared to HIS, which directly reflects loss sensitivity, AE may be less effective at identifying
uninformative or redundant heads, potentially resulting in lower performance. This suggests that AE
alone is insufficient as a reliable pruning criterion.
In contrast, pruning based on either HIS or HIES produces more stable and competitive results.
For instance, on SST-2 (Figure 1a), the performance of HIS and HIES is comparable to pruning 50%.
However, in 60% pruning level, HIES outperforms HIS by approximately 5%. A more significant

6 Expected Outcomes and Contributions
6.1 Potential for Performance Improvement
HIS is a widely used metric quantifying a head’s importance by measuring the expected change in loss
when the head is masked, but it has several limitations. First, it heavily relies on gradient magnitudes,
potentially overestimating the importance of heads exhibiting temporarily large gradients. Second,
in cases of head redundancy, multiple heads performing similar functions might each have high
HIS values, yet removing some has minimal impact on performance—a scenario HIS alone cannot
distinguish. Third, HIS lacks an explicit diversity constraint, failing to detect or penalize attention
collapse (where multiple heads converge on similar tokens, reducing contextual information), and is
insensitive to task- or layer-specific differences in head importance, making a single global threshold
suboptimal. To address these shortcomings, we integrate AE, which measures the uniformity of
each head’s attention distribution. High AE indicates that a head attends broadly across many
tokens—thereby leveraging richer contextual signals—whereas low AE signals that attention is
concentrated on a few tokens, potentially limiting information diversity.
By integrating HIS and AE through a weighted score, HIES retains heads that are both critical to loss
minimization and exhibit diverse attention. This joint criterion enables the pruning of heads that are
either redundant or overly focused, even if they show high HIS values—leading to more effective
compression without performance loss.
6.2 Impact of Pruning on Resource Efficiency (FLOPs)
We evaluate the impact of progressive head pruning on the TinyBERT_SST2 model’s inference
FLOPs, for pruning fractions ranging from 10% to 90%. As shown in Figure 2, under HIS-only
pruning, validation accuracy on SST-2 sharply degrades beyond a 42% pruning threshold; at this
point, total FLOPs savings amount to only ≈ 13% (i.e., total FLOPs ≈ 87%, attention-only FLOPs
≈ 58%). In contrast, HIES-based pruning preserves validation accuracy ≥ 80% up to a 60% pruning
ratio, at which point total FLOPs drop to ≈ 80% (a ≈ 20% reduction) and attention-only FLOPs to
≈ 42%. Extending the safe pruning limit from 42% to 60% thus yields an additional ≈ 7 percentage-
point reduction in total FLOPs and an additional ≈ 16 percentage-point reduction in attention-only
FLOPs. These results demonstrate that HIES sustains model performance while substantially boosting
computational efficiency within the attention submodules.
7 Further Research and Extensions
7.1 Applicability Across Model Architectures
The HIES pruning framework is broadly applicable to any Transformer architecture that provides
access to per-head attention activations and their corresponding gradients. Specifically, the method
assumes access to:
(i) attention weight distributions from each self-attention head during the forward pass
(ii) gradient signals with respect to these activations obtained via backpropagation
7

These two components are sufficient to compute the HIS through gradient-based sensitivity analysis
and AE via the entropy of the attention weight distribution.
The framework is model-agnostic and applicable to encoder-only models, decoder-only models, and
encoder–decoder models. In each case, forward and backward hooks can be registered to capture the
necessary activations and gradients during a standard evaluation or fine-tuning pass.
Beyond standard Transformers, the framework naturally extends to more recent variants and hybrid
models such as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Transformers, Mamba (state-space) architectures with
attention-like modules, and diffusion-based sequence models, provided that analogous attention
representations and gradient signals are accessible. This generality enables HIES to serve as a unified
pruning strategy across a broad spectrum of sequence modeling paradigms.
7.2 Integration of Dynamic Pruning Techniques During Fine-tuning
The HIES combination weight α can be treated as a differentiable parameter and jointly optimized
with model weights via stochastic gradient descent (SGD) by incorporating the HIES term into the
overall training loss. Given that:
HIESh(α) = α ˆIh + (1 − α) ˆEh
is differentiable with respect to α, its gradient can be computed and propagated alongside standard
model gradients. This enables α to dynamically adjust the relative influence of loss sensitivity (HIS)
and attention diversity (AE) in response to evolving training signals. Such adaptivity mitigates the
abrupt accuracy degradation often observed under fixed α values and aligns our method with prior
differentiable pruning frameworks Li et al. (2021); Cho et al. (2023) and dynamic sparse training
techniques Evci et al. (2020), resulting in a flexible, performance-preserving pruning paradigm.
7.3 Head Removal Patterns (Heatmap)
In this study, we evaluate the importance of all attention heads using AE, HIS, and their combination
HIES. Heads across all layers are globally ranked and pruned based on their scores, removing
the lowest-ranking ones according to a predefined ratio. However, this global strategy results in a
layer-wise imbalance—some layers lose multiple heads while others remain nearly intact.
In extreme cases, entire layers lose all their attention heads, effectively disabling their self-attention
mechanisms and impairing contextual information flow. To prevent such degradation, layer-wise
constraints (e.g., retaining at least one head per layer) are necessary to preserve structural integrity.
As shown in Figure 4– 7, a heatmap of head removal patterns reveals that pruning tends to concentrate
in specific layers, potentially disrupting representational balance and over-pruning sensitive regions.
These findings highlight the need for more structurally aware strategies. In particular, layer-aware or
adaptive pruning approaches can potentially better maintain model stability by accounting for the
functional role and relative importance of each layer.
8 Conclusion
This study exposes the limitations of pruning strategies that depend solely on HIS: as the pruning
ratio rises, accuracy deteriorates sharply, and the collapse of AE is overlooked. To overcome these
challenges, we propose Head Importance Entropy Score ( HIES)-based pruning method, which
merges AE with HIS via min–max normalization followed by a weighted sum controlled by α,
thereby reflecting both contribution and distribution diversity.
Experiments on GLUE (SST-2, MRPC, CoLA) and SQuAD v1.1 demonstrate that HIES-based
pruning preserves accuracy up to 8.9 percentage points better than HIS-only pruning and pushes the
safe pruning limit from 42 % to 60 %. This gain yields an additional 7 percentage-point reduction in
total FLOPs and over 16 percentage-point reduction in attention-module FLOPs without sacrificing
performance. Sensitivity analysis further shows that HIES remains stable across a broad α range
(0.5 ≤ α ≤ 0.9), mitigating hyperparameter-tuning overhead.
In sum, HIES retains the efficiency of HIS-centric pruning while incorporating AE to safeguard
post-pruning accuracy and model robustness, delivering tangible improvements in inference speed
and memory usage for mobile and on-device deployments.
